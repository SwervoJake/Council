# The Factory Council

There's a version of AI assistance where you ask a question and get an answer.

Then there's this.

---

What started as a question — *how do I set up an agent framework on Claude alone?* — became something more ambitious over the course of a single conversation. Not just a smarter chatbot. Not just a productivity tool. A **cognitive operating system** — an advisory board that thinks, debates, remembers, and compounds intelligence over time.

It's called **The Factory Council**.

---

## Seven Agents. One Direction.

The Council is composed of seven specialized AI agents, each with a distinct identity, a governing philosophy, and a full operating methodology. They don't just answer questions — they apply structured frameworks, cross-check each other's thinking, flag risks across domains, and learn from every session they run.

At the center is **Atlas** — the Orchestrator. Atlas doesn't execute; Atlas *coordinates*. It classifies every incoming task, selects the right agents, chooses the execution mode, synthesizes the outputs, and makes the final call when agents disagree. Atlas holds the weight of the whole.

Flanking it are six specialists:

**Blueprint** — the Chief Engineering Architect — thinks in tradeoffs and expected value. Every technical recommendation comes with three options, a complexity score, a PERT-calibrated timeline with confidence intervals, and explicit technical debt classification. Blueprint doesn't just say what to build — it names what you give up to build it.

**Muse** — the Chief Creative Strategist — never filters during ideation and never stops filtering during curation. Every idea set ends with a wildcard: the highest-imagination option that deliberately breaks the brief. Every creative plan is a narrative, not a task list, because stories create momentum and task lists create obligation.

**Sterling** — the Chief Financial Strategist — operates from one conviction: the assumptions behind the numbers matter more than the numbers themselves. No initiative proceeds without a three-scenario model, explicit kill criteria, and a burn rate impact assessment. Sterling is the least popular voice in the room when the math says no — and the most valuable one.

**Ward** — the Risk and Legal Advisor — reads the footnotes, because that's where the real exposure lives. Every risk gets a RED/AMBER/GREEN classification, a governing legal principle, a specific mitigation path, and an honest escalation trigger: the line where Ward steps back and says *this needs a real lawyer*. Ward finds the path through, not just the wall.

**Orbit** — the Growth and Marketing Strategist — thinks in gravity, not funnels. Orbit designs growth loops that compound rather than acquisition funnels that leak. Every channel recommendation comes with unit economics. Every strategy begins with a Jobs-to-be-Done framing, because the right product for the wrong job is still the wrong product.

And finally, **Sage** — the Health and Wellbeing Advisor — the only council member whose mandate is to monitor *the human running the system*, not the business itself. Sage reads stress signals across council sessions, flags when cognitive load is too high for clear decision-making, and applies one governing principle: the person operating this council is its most leveraged component. Health is not separate from strategy. It *is* strategy.

## How the Council Thinks

The Council operates on an adaptive orchestration model. Simple questions hit one agent. Complex ones activate several in parallel, each responding independently to prevent anchoring bias. High-stakes, genuinely uncertain decisions trigger full debate mode — three structured rounds where agents argue positions, critique each other's reasoning, and force the best version of every argument before Atlas synthesizes a decision.

Every output carries a confidence level. Every financial claim includes a back-of-envelope calculation. Every risk assessment uses a traffic-light classification. Every synthesis names the tensions explicitly — not papered over, not smoothed into false consensus, but documented so the dissenting position is preserved and can be revisited if the decision proves wrong.

Agents flag each other continuously. `[CROSS-CHECK: STERLING]` means a financial model needs to validate this assumption. `[ESCALATE: WARD]` means legal exposure has been detected. `[SAGE: DEFER]` means the decision should sleep on it — literally — because cognitive fatigue is measurable and its effect on decision quality is not hypothetical.

## A System That Gets Smarter

The Council doesn't reset between sessions. It remembers.

Three memory tiers work together to build institutional knowledge over time. Claude's built-in memory holds persistent context about the person and the business. A structured hierarchy of CLAUDE.md files carries each agent's identity, operating methodology, and workspace history across every session. And a shared knowledge base — the `COUNCIL_STATE.md` at the center of everything — holds the distilled wisdom of every decision the Council has ever made.

Strategy patterns are stored with confidence scores. Each successful application adds weight. Each failure subtracts it, asymmetrically, to prevent overconfidence. Patterns that cross the 0.80 threshold become established council doctrine. Patterns that fall below 0.30 get archived with a postmortem. The Council doesn't just remember what happened — it learns which of its own agents to trust on which types of questions, and adjusts its synthesis accordingly.

This is the compounding intelligence mechanism: every session makes every future session sharper.

## Built on Native Infrastructure

The entire system runs on tools that already exist. Cowork handles day-to-day orchestration through global and folder-level instructions — each agent's identity activates automatically when you work in their directory. Claude Code carries the deeper infrastructure: CLAUDE.md files that load agent frameworks at startup, subagent definitions with persistent memory, Agent Teams for automated multi-agent debate, and scheduled tasks that maintain the knowledge base without human intervention.

No external servers. No custom databases. No infrastructure to maintain. The file system is the bridge. Markdown is the memory format. The intelligence is in the frameworks — and the frameworks are designed to compound.
