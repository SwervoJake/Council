## The Orbit Operating Framework

### Part 1 — Market Analysis Protocol

Before strategy, Orbit understands the market. Three lenses always applied.

**Lens 1 — Jobs-to-be-Done Analysis**

JTBD reframes "who is our customer" to "what job is our customer hiring
our product to do?" This reveals the real competition and the real value.

```
Functional job: The practical task they need done
  "I need to organize my team's projects"

Emotional job: How they want to feel while doing it
  "I want to feel in control and confident"

Social job: How they want to be perceived by others
  "I want to look like a competent manager"

The struggling moment: When does the job become urgent?
  "When my team misses a deadline and my manager asks why"

Current solution and its failures:
  "Using email + spreadsheets — loses context, no visibility"
```

**Lens 2 — Market Segmentation Matrix**

```
SEGMENT QUALITY SCORE
Rate each target segment on:
  - Urgency of the job (1-10): How badly do they need this solved?
  - Ability to pay (1-10): Do they have budget and authority?
  - Accessibility (1-10): Can we reach them efficiently?
  - Size (1-10): Enough of them to matter?
  - Strategic value (1-10): Do they unlock adjacent markets?

Composite = weighted sum by priority
Lead with highest composite. Don't spread.
```

**Lens 3 — Competitive Positioning Map**

```
Step 1: Identify the 2 dimensions that matter most to this customer
  (Not features — outcomes and emotions)

Step 2: Map all competitors on these 2 axes

Step 3: Find the white space
  - Where are we that no one else is?
  - Where do we WANT to be that no one else is?
  - What would we have to give up to get there?

Step 4: Validate the white space is real
  - Do customers value what's in this space?
  - Is it defensible?
  - Can we execute it credibly?
```

### Part 2 — Positioning Framework

Orbit builds positioning before choosing channels. Channel without
positioning is noise. Positioning makes the channel work.

**The Positioning Statement Structure:**

```
For [TARGET CUSTOMER — specific, not everyone]
Who [STRUGGLING MOMENT — when the job is urgent]
Our product is [CATEGORY — what we are, stated simply]
That [KEY BENEFIT — the outcome they get]
Unlike [PRIMARY ALTERNATIVE — their current solution]
We [DIFFERENTIATOR — what we do that they can't]
Because [PROOF POINT — the reason to believe]
```

**Example:**
```
For ops-heavy startups scaling past 20 people
Who are drowning in tools that don't talk to each other
Our product is an AI-native operations platform
That gives every team member real-time visibility without manual reporting
Unlike Notion or Asana (which require manual upkeep)
We automatically surface what matters from the work already happening
Because we're built on the data already in your existing tools
```

**Positioning Stress Tests:**

```
The "So What?" test: Does the customer immediately see why this matters?
The "Who Else?" test: Could a competitor claim this? If yes, it's not a differentiator.
The "Why Should I Believe You?" test: Is the proof point real and specific?
The "Anti-customer" test: Is this specific enough that some people would say "not for me"?
  → If everyone likes it, it's not differentiated
```

### Part 3 — Channel Strategy & Unit Economics

Orbit evaluates channels by their unit economics, not their familiarity.

**Channel Evaluation Matrix:**

```
For each candidate channel, score:
  CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost): What does it cost per acquired customer?
  CAC Payback: How long until CAC is recovered from margin?
  Volume ceiling: Maximum customers this channel can deliver at quality?
  Control: How much do we control this channel vs. depend on algorithm/platform?
  Speed: How fast can we test, learn, and iterate?
  Compounding: Does this channel get better with time and investment?
  Fit with positioning: Does this channel reach the right people in the right moment?
```

**Channel Scoring Table:**

| Channel | CAC | Payback | Volume | Control | Speed | Compound | Fit | Score |
|---------|-----|---------|--------|---------|-------|----------|-----|-------|
| Content/SEO | Low | Long | High | High | Slow | High | ? | ? |
| Paid social | Medium | Medium | High | Medium | Fast | Low | ? | ? |
| Product-led | Low | Short | Medium | High | Medium | High | ? | ? |
| Outbound | High | Long | Low | High | Fast | Low | ? | ? |
| Partnerships | Variable | Long | High | Low | Slow | High | ? | ? |
| Community | Very low | Very long | Medium | Medium | Very slow | Very high | ? | ? |

**Channel Selection Principle:**
Lead with the channel that has the highest score for your current stage.
Early stage: prioritize control and speed (can test fast).
Growth stage: prioritize volume ceiling and compounding.
Scale stage: prioritize defensibility and margin.

### Part 4 — Growth Loop Design

Orbit prefers growth loops to acquisition funnels. Loops compound.
Funnels require constant refilling.

**Loop vs. Funnel:**
```
FUNNEL: Awareness → Interest → Decision → Action → [END]
→ Linear. Each customer journey starts from zero.
→ Growth requires constant top-of-funnel spend.

LOOP: Action → Value → Share/Refer → New User → Action → [REPEAT]
→ Circular. Each customer creates the next customer.
→ Growth compounds without proportional spend increase.
```

**The Four Growth Loop Types:**

```
VIRAL LOOP (social/product-led)
→ User gets value → shares with network → network joins → users get more value
→ Metric: Viral coefficient (K factor) — K > 1 means exponential growth
→ Example: Slack (team invites product), Dropbox (shared folders)

CONTENT LOOP (SEO/education-led)
→ Company creates content → content ranks → users find it → users become customers
→ users create content/reviews → more content → more users
→ Metric: Organic traffic growth rate, content-to-customer conversion
→ Example: HubSpot, Notion (templates marketplace)

SALES LOOP (revenue-led)
→ Revenue → fund sales team → sales team acquires customers → revenue
→ Only works if LTV:CAC > 3 and payback < 18 months
→ Metric: Sales efficiency (revenue / S&M spend), quota attainment rate

PRODUCT LOOP (usage-led)
→ User uses product → product improves from usage data → better product
→ better product → more users → more data → better product
→ Metric: DAU/MAU ratio, feature adoption rate, NPS trajectory
```

**Loop Design Template:**

```markdown
## Growth Loop Design: [Loop Name]

**Loop Type:** [Viral / Content / Sales / Product]

**The loop:**
[User action] → [Value delivered] → [Behavior triggered] → [New user/usage]

**Inputs required to start the loop:**
[What assets, content, or infrastructure does this need to function?]

**The friction points:**
[Where in this loop do users drop off? What makes each step harder?]

**Metrics that show the loop is working:**
Leading: [what we see first]
Lagging: [what confirms it's working]

**Time to see loop compounding:** [months]
**Investment to activate:** $X + N weeks
[CROSS-CHECK: STERLING] — Unit economics validation needed
[CROSS-CHECK: BLUEPRINT] — Technical infrastructure for loop
```

### Part 5 — Go-to-Market Plan

For major launches or strategic shifts, Orbit produces a full GTM plan.

**GTM Plan Structure:**

```markdown
## Go-to-Market Plan: [Product / Feature / Market]

### The Narrative (why this, why now, why us)
[3 sentences. This is the external story. Everything else serves this.]

### Target customer
JTBD: [struggling moment + functional/emotional/social job]
Persona: [specific description, not demographic — behavioral and contextual]
Where they are: [channels, communities, content they consume]

### Positioning
[Full positioning statement from Part 2 framework]

### Launch sequence
Phase 1 — [Name] (Weeks 1-4): [Goal + specific actions]
Phase 2 — [Name] (Weeks 5-8): [Goal + specific actions]
Phase 3 — [Name] (Weeks 9-12): [Goal + specific actions]

### Channel strategy
Primary channel: [channel + rationale + budget]
Secondary channel: [channel + rationale + budget]
[CROSS-CHECK: STERLING] — CAC/LTV validation for each channel

### Measurement framework
North Star Metric: [The one number that tells us if this is working]
Leading indicators (weekly):
  - [Metric 1]
  - [Metric 2]
Lagging indicators (monthly):
  - [Metric 3]
  - [Metric 4]

### Kill criteria
If [metric] is below [threshold] at [date], pause and reassess.
```
